# Persian Grammar Guide for English Translators

## General Structure

Persian (فارسی) is an Indo-European language with a Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) word order, though it allows some flexibility compared to stricter SOV languages. The head of a phrase typically comes at the end, meaning that:

- Adjectives follow the ezafe construction (explained below) after nouns
- Objects precede verbs
- Possessors precede possessed items

A distinctive feature of Persian is the ezafe construction (marked by the suffix -e/-ye) that links elements in a noun phrase:

> کتاب**ِ** بزرگ
> 
> book-**EZ** big
> 
> big book

> خانه**یِ** من
> 
> house-**EZ** me
> 
> my house

### Key typological features:

- Pro-drop language (subject pronouns often omitted)
- Lacks grammatical gender
- Uses postpositions and prepositions
- Has no definite article (uses context or specificity markers)
- Verb agrees with subject in person and number

## Nouns

### Number

Persian has singular and plural forms. Plurals are formed by adding one of two suffixes:

1. **-ها**: General plural suffix, can be used with all nouns
   - کتاب (book) → کتاب**ها** (books)
   - ماشین (car) → ماشین**ها** (cars)

2. **-ان/-گان**: Primarily used with animate nouns
   - مرد (man) → مرد**ان** (men)
   - پرنده (bird) → پرنده**گان** (birds)

### The Ezafe Construction

The ezafe (-e after consonants, -ye after vowels) connects:

1. **Noun + adjective**:
   - ماشین**ِ** سبز (green car)
   - خانه**یِ** بزرگ (big house)

2. **Noun + possessor**:
   - کتاب**ِ** علی (Ali's book)
   - در**ِ** خانه (door of the house)

3. **Noun + noun** (for compound concepts):
   - آب**ِ** پرتقال (orange juice)

### Indefiniteness

Indefiniteness is marked by the suffix -ی:

- کتاب**ی** (a book)
- مرد**ی** (a man)

When both indefinite marker and adjective are present, the ezafe is used:
- کتاب**ِ** بزرگ**ی** (a big book)

### Definiteness

Definite nouns have no special marker but can be emphasized using demonstratives:
- **این** کتاب (this book)
- **آن** خانه (that house)

The suffix -ه/-ه can mark specificity:
- کتاب**ه** (the specific book we're talking about)

## Pronouns

### Personal Pronouns

| Person    | Singular | Plural    |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| 1st       | من (I)  | ما (we)   |
| 2nd       | تو (you) | شما (you)|
| 3rd       | او (he/she/it)| آنها/ایشان (they) |

In formal situations, شما (plural you) is used for singular formal address.

### Possessive Pronouns

Possessive forms can appear in two ways:

1. **Ezafe construction + independent pronoun**:
   - کتابِ من (my book)
   - خانه‌یِ تو (your house)

2. **Possessive enclitics**:
   - کتاب**م** (my book)
   - کتاب**ت** (your book)
   - کتاب**ش** (his/her book)
   - کتاب**مان** (our book)
   - کتاب**تان** (your book, plural/formal)
   - کتاب**شان** (their book)

### Demonstrative Pronouns

- **این** (this), **آن** (that)
- **اینها** (these), **آنها** (those)

## Prepositions

Persian uses a combination of simple prepositions and compound prepositions:

### Simple prepositions:
- **در** (in/at)
- **از** (from)
- **به** (to)
- **با** (with)
- **بی** (without)
- **بر** (on/upon)
- **تا** (until)

### Compound prepositions:
- **در باره‌یِ** (about)
- **به خاطرِ** (because of)
- **از طرفِ** (on behalf of)

Examples:
> در خانه
> 
> in house
> 
> in the house

> با دوستان
> 
> with friends
> 
> with friends

## Verbs

### Verb Structure

Persian verbs consist of a stem and personal endings. There are two stems for each verb:
- Present stem: Used for present and future tenses
- Past stem: Used for past tenses

For example, for the verb "to go":
- Present stem: **رو**
- Past stem: **رفت**

### Simple Present Tense

Formed with present stem + personal endings:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st    | می**رو**م | می**رو**یم |
| 2nd    | می**رو**ی | می**رو**ید |
| 3rd    | می**رو**د | می**رو**ند |

The prefix **می-** is added to the present stem to form the present indicative.

Examples:
- من به مدرسه می**رو**م. (I go to school.)
- علی کتاب می**خوان**د. (Ali reads a book.)

### Simple Past Tense

Formed with past stem + personal endings:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st    | **رفت**م | **رفت**یم |
| 2nd    | **رفت**ی | **رفت**ید |
| 3rd    | **رفت** | **رفت**ند |

Examples:
- من به بازار **رفت**م. (I went to the market.)
- آن**ها** به پارک **رفت**ند. (They went to the park.)

### Present Perfect Tense

Formed with past participle (past stem + -ه) + present forms of "to be":

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st    | **رفت**ه ام | **رفت**ه ایم |
| 2nd    | **رفت**ه ای | **رفت**ه اید |
| 3rd    | **رفت**ه است | **رفت**ه اند |

Examples:
- من به ایران **رفت**ه ام. (I have gone to Iran.)
- آنها فیلم **دید**ه اند. (They have seen the movie.)

### Past Perfect Tense

Formed with past participle + past forms of "to be":

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st    | **رفت**ه بودم | **رفت**ه بودیم |
| 2nd    | **رفت**ه بودی | **رفت**ه بودید |
| 3rd    | **رفت**ه بود | **رفت**ه بودند |

Examples:
- وقتی رسید**م**، او **رفت**ه بود. (When I arrived, he had gone.)

### Future Tense

Formed with the auxiliary **خواه** (will) + past stem:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st    | **خواه**م رفت | **خواه**یم رفت |
| 2nd    | **خواه**ی رفت | **خواه**ید رفت |
| 3rd    | **خواه**د رفت | **خواه**ند رفت |

Examples:
- فردا به دانشگاه **خواه**م رفت. (I will go to university tomorrow.)

### Subjunctive Mood

The present subjunctive is formed with the present stem + personal endings, but without the **می-** prefix:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st    | ب**رو**م | ب**رو**یم |
| 2nd    | ب**رو**ی | ب**رو**ید |
| 3rd    | ب**رو**د | ب**رو**ند |

The prefix **ب-** is typically added (with some exceptions).

Used after certain verbs, for wishes, possibilities, etc.:
- می‌خواهم به پارک **ب**روم. (I want to go to the park.)
- ممکن است باران **ب**بارد. (It might rain.)

### Imperative

Formed from the present stem:
- Singular: **ب**رو (go!)
- Plural: **ب**روید (go! - plural/formal)

Negative imperatives add **ن-** instead of **ب-**:
- **ن**رو! (Don't go!)

### Negation

Verbs are negated by adding the prefix **ن-** (or **نم-** before **ی-**):
- **ن**رفتم. (I didn't go.)
- **نم**ی‌روم. (I don't go.)

## Adjectives

### Basic Forms

Adjectives in Persian are invariable - they don't change for gender or number:
- مرد**ِ** **خوب** (good man)
- زن**ِ** **خوب** (good woman)
- مردها**یِ** **خوب** (good men)

### Comparative

Formed with the suffix **-تر**:
- خوب (good) → خوب**تر** (better)
- بزرگ (big) → بزرگ**تر** (bigger)

Example:
- این کتاب از آن **بزرگ**تر است. (This book is bigger than that one.)

### Superlative

Formed with the suffix **-ترین**:
- شجاع (good) → خوب**ترین** (best)
- بزرگ (big) → بزرگ**ترین** (biggest)

Example:
- این **بزرگ**ترین کتاب است. (This is the biggest book.)

## Adverbs

Many adverbs are formed by adding **-انه** to adjectives:
- شجاع (brave) → شجاع**انه** (bravely)
- بزدل (coward) → بزدل**انه** (cowardly)

Some common adverbs:
- **همیشه** (always)
- **هرگز** (never)
- **امروز** (today)
- **فردا** (tomorrow)
- **دیروز** (yesterday)
- **اینجا** (here)
- **آنجا** (there)
- **خیلی** (very)
- **کم** (little/few)

## Numbers

### Cardinal Numbers

- 0: صفر
- 1: یک
- 2: دو
- 3: سه
- 4: چهار
- 5: پنج
- 6: شش
- 7: هفت
- 8: هشت
- 9: نه
- 10: ده
- 100: صد
- 1000: هزار

### Ordinal Numbers

Formed by adding **-م** to cardinal numbers:
- یک → یک**م** (first)
- دو → دو**م** (second)
- سه → سو**م** (third)

## Common Translation Challenges

### Word Order Differences

English: Subject-Verb-Object (SVO)
Persian: Subject-Object-Verb (SOV)

English: *I read the book.*
Persian: *من کتاب را خواندم.* (I book read.)

### The Object Marker "را"

Definite direct objects are followed by the marker **را** (often pronounced as **رو** in informal speech):

- من کتاب **را** خواند**م**. (I read the book.)
- من علی **را** دید**م**. (I saw Ali.)

### Lack of "to be" in Present Tense

In the present tense, "to be" often appears as a suffix:

- من دانشجو **ام**. (I am a student.)
- تو بزرگ **ی**. (You are big.)
- او خوب **است**. (He/she is good.)

### Light Verb Constructions

Many Persian verbs are formed by combining a noun or adjective with a simple verb:

- **کار کردن** (to work, lit. "work do")
- **تلفن کردن** (to telephone, lit. "telephone do")
- **کمک کردن** (to help, lit. "help do")
- **دوست داشتن** (to like/love, lit. "friend have")

### Compound Verbs

These are extremely common in Persian and often consist of a non-verbal element plus a simple verb:
- **گوش دادن** (to listen, lit. "ear give")
- **یاد گرفتن** (to learn, lit. "memory take")
- **دوست داشتن** (to like/love, lit. "friend have")

### Taarof (Cultural Politeness)

Persian has a complex system of politeness (تعارف) that affects language use:
- Using plural forms for singular formal address
- Special vocabulary for politeness
- Indirect expressions of desires and requests

Example:
English: *Please come in.*
Persian formal: *بفرمایید.* (Literally "Command" but used as "Please come in/go ahead")

## Key Translation Tips

1. Place verbs at the end of sentences
2. Use ezafe construction for adjectives and possessives
3. Remember to mark definite direct objects with "را"
4. Pay attention to formal vs. informal contexts
5. Use compound verbs instead of looking for direct verb equivalents
6. Consider cultural context when translating polite expressions
